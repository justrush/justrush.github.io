The Situation

Project Kuiper — Amazon's $10B+ low-Earth-orbit satellite constellation — needed its first prototype satellites in production within 13 months. But 80–85% of production BOMs, supplier contracts, demand forecasting, and materials planning lived in scattered spreadsheets and engineering tools. The permanent ERP was 12–18 months out. The launch timeline was at risk.

The Constraint

I was the newest member of the Hardware Supply Management team, with limited full-stack development experience — and three months to deliver a bridge platform covering demand-supply balancing, purchasing, inventory tracking, and supplier management.

What I Did

  • Spent three weeks mapping the end-to-end process and writing the Business Requirements Document that became the platform's cornerstone — user stories, risk matrices, and system requirements aligned across five supply-chain functions.
  • Taught myself AWS Amplify and JavaScript from documentation and forums, then built the full stack solo: product-tree search, supplier and COTS BOM search, purchasing reconciliation, and a demand aggregator.
  • Embedded a dedicated Tableau server for real-time KPI reporting to internal leadership (up to VP level) and external contract manufacturers.

The Results

  • Launched in 7 weeks — against typical estimates of 28–36 weeks — optimizing 20+ key processes across 6 parallel supply-chain functions.
  • Demand aggregation cut BOM reconciliation by 85% and improved forecast accuracy by 32%, tripling the program's span of control.
  • Real-time visibility across 37 PCBA builds surfaced 15+ high-risk components, driving a "Hot Parts" strategy and contract revisions that protected the prototype launch schedule.

The Situation

Amazon's compliance tool (internally, "ARNOLD") suppressed 1.1M product listings a year for regulatory violations — leaving 28M units and $115M in goods stranded in fulfillment centers. Retail teams couldn't see why products were pulled, operations couldn't prioritize removals, and 2M+ cubic feet of warehouse space sat wasted.

What I Built

  • Normalization Cost Model — quantified the problem for the first time: each week of suppression cost 1.42 weeks of sales recovery, and past 180 days a product had an 82% chance of becoming permanently obsolete. This analysis won $1.2M in funding for a dedicated stranded-inventory team.
  • ARNOLD Lookup Table (ALT) — nine Tableau dashboards on nine Redshift tables giving 3,200+ trained business partners self-service suppression research, with 20+ training videos captioned in 22 languages. Now part of mandatory worldwide Retail onboarding.
  • ARNOLD Heat Maps (AHM) — created the Disposition Cycle Time KPI, root-caused a catalog flag that treated every product as a safety recall, and drove the code change that let fulfillment centers prioritize genuinely hazardous inventory.

The Results

  • Safety-related disposition time fell from 78 days to 9 (−88%); non-safety from 65 to 12.
  • Stranded retail COGS fell 71% ($35.3M → $10.3M); $20.2M recovered from vendors under contract terms.
  • Holding costs dropped 85% and 1.87M cubic feet of bin space returned to productive use — roughly $107M in combined three-year impact.

The Situation

Amazon's prepared-meals logistics network (HMR) was heading into 139% year-over-year volume growth — from 10 delivery routes to 30, and from 90 weekly scheduling appointments to a projected 375+. The choice: hire more people, or make the existing team dramatically more capable.

What I Did

  • When the platform team deprioritized the bulk-upload feature we needed, I built the workaround: a SQL query publishing daily PO reports, an Excel layer standardizing the data, and a Selenium script that drove the scheduling website automatically — "ScheduleBot."
  • In parallel, I standardized the network's 11 core operational deliverables (30 key processes) into SOPs and translated them into a Transportation Management System business-requirements document, submitted inside the annual planning deadline.

The Results

  • ScheduleBot cut scheduling labor by 73%+ and scheduling errors by 97%, saving 915 labor hours (~$56K) over ten months of service — built entirely from tools already on the desktop.
  • The TMS proposal was approved: forecast $675K in annual savings, a 233% span-of-control increase (1:15 → 1:50), and 41 hours of weekly reporting eliminated.
  • The documentation work itself surfaced nine process improvements that reduced operational load by 25% before any technology shipped.

Green City: the migration two vendors called impossible

The Situation

Green City Heating & Air Conditioning — a Seattle-area family HVAC company — ran on an undocumented custom WordPress CRM costing $10K a month to keep alive and bleeding 140 staff-hours a month to workarounds. A professional migration to ServiceTitan had already failed after four months and $50K in fees. I was brought in as the last resort.

What I Did

  • Reverse-engineered the CRM's 67 database tables and 1.3M rows, eliminating 40 redundant tables and mapping the remaining 20 into Housecall Pro's data model.
  • Built a deterministic JSON/CSV API pipeline (MD5-keyed) to reliably link 4,374 customers to 9,439 jobs and 8,997 estimates — including the many-to-one relationships that broke the previous attempt.
  • Layered automation on top: Make.com, n8n, and LLM workflows for estimate follow-ups, dispatch, invoice sync, appointment reminders, and Gusto payroll.

The Results

  • $20K+ in first-year savings across IT, labor, and automation; office admin workload down ~60% (20 → 8 hours weekly).
  • Automated reminders cut no-shows by up to 60%, protecting $8–10K in annual revenue; technicians gained ~$15K in billable capacity.
  • The business now runs on a scalable SaaS foundation with APIs, real-time KPIs, and online booking — positioned for 30%+ revenue growth without added overhead.

The Situation

A mid-market electrical distributor engaged me to find out why growth kept stalling. Over a focused three-day evaluation I traced their sales cycle, cash-to-cash cycle, data flow, and process management end to end.

What I Found & Recommended

  • Warehouse & inventory: barcode scanning and EDI inside their existing P21 ERP — 780 hours/year recovered for a $20K investment.
  • AP/AR: automated three-way invoice matching and electronic funds transfer — 1,040 hours/year for $15K.
  • Sales & quoting: a customer portal with automated quotes and live vendor APIs — 1,560 hours/year for $25K.
  • Plus automated shipping scheduling, standardized project management, and structured weekly/monthly/quarterly business reviews.

The Results

  • A costed, sequenced roadmap: $83K total investment returning $323,700 annually — a 390% first-year ROI.
  • Every recommendation tied to the metrics it would move: inventory accuracy, quote-to-order cycle time, days payable outstanding, and on-time delivery.
